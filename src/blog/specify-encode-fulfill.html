Specify-encode-fulfill: the distilled essence of test-driven development

If there are intelligent alients on other planets, and if they've achieved a level intellectual advancement at least on par with our own, then these aliens surely will have independently discovered and invented many of the same things that we humans have.

To start with a trivial example, they will surely have discovered basic mathematical truths such as the fact that two plus two equals four. They will also have discovered the Pythagorean theorem, the existence of prime numbers, and so on. Since there's only one version of the truth, any extraterrestrial mathematicians will not have found some variant of the Pythagorean theorem or prime numbers which we consider to be factorable; they will have found, by necessity, facts which agree with ours.

I would also bet that any extraterrestrial civilization that has invented science has invented essentially the same scientific method as ours: take a stab at the explanation for some currently-unsolved mystery (e.g. "what are the stars?") and then subject your explanation to criticism to try to find reasons it could be wrong, and if the criticisms fail, then your explanation is tentatively accepted as true. (Side note: if this description of science sounds a bit off to you, then you may share my misfortune of having been taught about science by schoolteachers instead of by Karl Popper.) The reason why all civilizations' versions of science must be very similar is because the scientific method can't be varied very much and still work. This is the same reason why non-scientific "ways of knowing" here on Earth have failed to explain many of the phenomena that science has explained successfully. There is one and only one reality, and one and only one reliable method of determining what's true.

If there are exo-civilizations that have invented (or perhaps we should say discovered) science, then they surely will have discovered computation as well. Computation doesn't require a computer, of course—the word computer originally referred to a person whose job it was to carry out computations, and people have been computing for millenia—but if you know about computation and you know about machines then it might only be a matter of time before you put the two together and create a machine which performs computations automatically. A computer needs programs of course, and programs need a programmer, so if there are extraterrestrial computers then there must be extraterrestrial programmers.

The same way that alien mathematicians must have discovered essential mathematical ideas like arithmetic and prime numbers and such, alien programmers must have discovered essential ideas in programming like loops, conditionals, version control, and—hear me out—test-driven development. I realize that this proposition might sound unserious and fanciful, but give me some rope and read on.

To be clear, I don't think there are aliens out there doing specifically Kent Beck's red-green-refactor TDD, not any more than I might imagine there might be aliens practicing specifically Leibniz' flavor of calculus. The TDD we do here on Earth (and calculus for that matter) is a mix the essential and the incidental. I want to turn our attention now to what some of those essential and incidental ingredients are.

Kent Beck's Canon TDD

If one is looking for the authoritative definition of test-driven development, one could surely do worse than to consult the version designated canonical by the foremost progenitor of the practice. Here's the definition Kent Beck gives in a post called Canon TDD.

  1. Write a list of the test scenarios you want to cover
  2. Turn exactly one item on the list into an actual, concrete, runnable test
  3. Change the code to make the test (& all previous tests) pass (adding items to the list as you discover them)
  4. Optionally refactor to improve the implementation design
  5. Until the list is empty, go back to #2

When I teach TDD, I used to teach red-green-refactor, a version of red-green-refactor which matches Kent Beck's Canon TDD almost exactly. But I no longer do—or rather, that's not where I start (even though I agree with it heartily). There are three reasons for this.

The first is that I think Canon TDD contains certain details which, although essential and correct in themselves, are not necessary in order to understand the top-level essence of TDD, and in fact I feel that these details distract from this essence and create possible points of confusion. (Soon we'll touch on what exactly these details are.)

The second reason is because I think Canon TDD contains a certain element (refactoring) which is not essential to TDD, or at least, whose essentiality is debatable. If extraterrestrial civilizations have discovered TDD, it's hard to imagine that all of them would consider refactoring to be an essential part of it. To be clear, I think taking out the optional refactoring step would make TDD significantly worse, but I don't think doing so would take away anything essential.

Lastly, I think Canon TDD's third step (change the code to make the tests pass) is slightly mistaken (or at least imprecise) in a way that we'll look at more closely later. None of this is to say that I think Canon TDD is wrong (except for perhaps the small imprecision in step 3), it's just not what I personally choose to teach first. When I teach TDD, I aim to convey the minimal essence of TDD, and at a high level of abstraction. I want as little room for misunderstanding as possible.

The specify-encode-fulfill loop

My preferred way of conveying the essence of test-driven development, the specify-encode-fulfill loop, goes like this:

  1. Specify: Decide on the specifications for what you want to build
  2. Encode: Encode your specifications in the form of automated tests
  3. Fulfill: Implement the behavior to fulfill those specifications
  4. Repeat

Clearly, specify-encode-fulfill (SEF) is not that different from Canon TDD. My aim here is not to create something revolutionarily different, only to present the stripped-down essence of TDD in a way that's easy to understand. Think of it like a new photograph of the same subject, but from a slightly different angle and with different lighting. I'm not trying to create a new idea, only to present an existing idea in a new way.

Another aim of SEF is to build into the definition of TDD the rationale behind it, so that instead of being a mystery, the rationale behind the process can hardly be missed. Many people are confused by red-green-refactor's seemingly counterintuitive practice of writing a failing test before writing the code to make it pass. To some it seems like a superstitious, quasi-religious ritual. By omitting the relatively abstract "red" and "green" steps as well as the (in my view) non-essential "refactor" step, I hope to show that starting with a failing test is really starting with an unfulfilled specification. Of course each specification starts off unfulfilled. How could it be any other way? And of course the specification comes first and the fulfillment of the specification comes after. How could it be any other way? It's not superstition, it's natural, logical common sense.

But just because we start high-level doesn't mean we have to stay there. Specify-encode-fulfill is compatible with lower levels of abstraction. We can go down a level and change it from an explanation to an algorithm:

  1. Specify: Decide on the specifications for what you want to build
  2. Encode: Encode exactly one of your specifications in the form of an automated test
  3. Fulfill: Implement the (absolute bare minimum) behavior to fulfill that specification
  4. Until the list is empty, go back to #2

In fact, we can map specify-encode-fulfill directly onto Kent Beck's Canon TDD:

  1. Specify: Write a list of the test scenarios you want to cover
  2. Encode: Turn exactly one item on the list into an actual, concrete, runnable test
  3. Fulfill: Change the code to make the test (& all previous tests) pass (adding items to the list as you discover them)
  4. Until the list is empty, go back to #2

Specify

One of the most common mistakes I see from programmers and even managers is a failure to decide what to do before they do it. Nobody wants to waste time deciding what to do, they just want to do it. These impatient programmers and managers often even dish out wise-sounding "advice" as a counterargument such as "don't get stuck in analysis paralysis" and "perfect is the enemy of the good". They then proceed to waste weeks or months of expensive engineering time in order to save a few days' worth of planning. It's nuts, but it's the norm.

Test is a bit of an unfortunate name for a test. It has given a lot of programmers the notion that the main idea behind testing is inspection or verification, which it's not. The main idea behind testing is to force our programs' behavior to conform to our desired specifications. In fact, some people, including myself, often refer to tests as executable specifications. I think this is a much better name than test. Another, perhaps even better term might be automated assertions. We're making assertions about our programs' behavior, and doing so in an automated way. From here on I'll use "test", "executable specification" and "automated assertion" interchangeably.

In science and philosophy (and in general), the creation of knowledge involves conjectures and refutations. Someone makes a hypothesis, and then someone else (or the same person) makes attempts via argument or experiment to refute the hypothesis, and if the attempts at refutation succeed, then the hypothesis is proven false. The inverse is not true; if attempts to refute a hypothesis fail, that does not necessarily mean that the hypothesis is true. If the hypothesis seems like a good explanation and all attempts to refute it fail, then it is accepted as true, but only tentatively, since we can never definitively prove a hypothesis true, we can only fail to to prove it false.

In science, we start with gold-speckled dirt and we pass it through a filter to find the gold, and then we throw out the dirt. In programming it's the reverse: we start with the gold, our sacred specifications, which are correct and authoritative simply because we say they are. The challenge isn't to get the gold out of the dirt; the challenge is to keep the dirt off of our clean white sheet of specifications. What's the dirt in this scenario? It's anything we didn't specify. In the same way that a hypothesis can be proven false but never true, automated tests can prove the presence of the right behavior but never the absence of the wrong behavior.
