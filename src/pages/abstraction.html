Abstraction

In my home office, on a stand across the room from my desk, I have a Fender Stratocaster. It's made of wood, steel and plastic. If you pluck a string, it makes a sound. It's a very concrete object.

Not all physical entities are so concrete. A wave, for example, has an existence independent of the material that makes it up. When a surfer catches a wave and rides it to shore, the wave is made of completely different water at the end than at the beginning, even though it was the same wave the whole time.

My guitar is concrete and a wave is abstract—at least, a little bit abstract. It might be more fitting to say that a wave is not absolutely concrete. A wave is more abstract than a guitar but it isn't nearly as abstract as, say, the Pythagorean theorem or the concept of social status. Concreteness and abstractness come in degrees.

A wave, because it is abstract, is an abstraction. An abstraction is what results from the act of throwing away incidental, lower-level information (e.g. the water in a wave) and focusing on essential, higher-level information (e.g. the wave itself). Note that it doesn't take much abstractness at all for something to qualify as an abstraction. Tornadoes, rivers, glaciers, beaches, piles of sand and forest fires are all examples of abstractions, despite the fact that they're quite physically formidable.

Even though the guitar in my office is concrete (as a concept—it's not made out of literal concrete), the concept of a guitar is an abstraction. If I tell you that I play the guitar, you're not going to ask me which guitar I mean. You and I both understand that I just mean "the guitar" in an abstract sense. Humanity has exactly one concept that means guitar, and that's why I say that I play the guitar, not that I play a guitar.

I said earlier that a river is an abstraction. It's made of water, but no particular water. Its course can change from year to year. Like a wave, it has an existence independent of its material composition. Not only is each individual river an abstraction (meaning that, for example, the Mississippi river, with its ever-renewing waters and shifting course), but the concept of a river is an abstraction as well. The general concept of a river has an existence independent of any specific set of rivers. It's an abstraction on top of an abstraction.

If the general concept of a river is an abstraction, so must the general concept of a tree, and, as every programmer knows, we can invent an extremely abstract idea called tree which is useful for representing certain kinds of data. This is an abstraction on top of an abstraction on top of an abstraction—at least! If I so fancied, I could set my gaze on one specific oak tree in my front yard, throw away details to get the general concept of an oak tree, throw away more details to get the general species-agnostic concept of a tree, then throw away yet more details to get the concept of a tree data structure. Ironically, by throwing away information, we can make a concept more useful for a certain purpose. Your family's lineage can be nicely represented by an austere tree data structure; good luck trying to represent it using the oak tree in my front yard.

Many abstractions are also analogies. A tree data structure has a "root" node as well as "leaf" nodes. Email is analogous to "regular" mail, and a specific instance of an email is an abstraction over a collection of details such as sender, recipient, body and so forth. Abstractions can be nested (another analogy!) and combined, giving us a rich repertoire of ways to represent ideas.

Since abstractions can strip away unneeded details and compress big and complex ideas into small and convenient shorthands, they can save great expense in cognition and communication. Think about how many details are packed into the sentence "His AI startup IPO'd." How would you explain the meaning of this to, say, a farmer from ancient Mesopotamia? You would soon exhaust both his short-term memory and his patience. Abstractions not only make thinking and communicating more efficient; they make the difference between whether an idea is even possible to work with or not. That's why it's my opinion that abstraction is the most important idea in software.
